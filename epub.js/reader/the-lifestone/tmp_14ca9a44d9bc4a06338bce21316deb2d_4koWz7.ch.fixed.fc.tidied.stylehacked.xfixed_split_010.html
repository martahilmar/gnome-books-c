Chapter 5

The time finally had arrived, Nailstead's army had fully entered the field of battle, they crawled, ran or slithered their bellies mostly held close to the ground. There were some that even hopped moving forward on deformed legs towards my meager looking defenses, in anticipation of blood letting and the victory promised to them by Nailstead the dark one. They had been lulled, I believed by the few soldiers dug in ready that apparently awaited them. The army, my army rallied, preparing itself for the desperate assault to come, an assault that it alone would have to rebuff. I climbed down from my rock plinth, now hidden from sight by the bricks that made up its wall.

To my left a creature with wings and a large hooked beak flapped idly at the air, eager to carry its message to the Dwarf army hidden out there to the right. Equally, an identical messenger waited to my left, but a bit more patient, an older bird, that was obvious by its plumage displaying its faded colours, but with a wiser more experienced head on it shoulders, it also awaited my command. A command that would send both flying to left and right of the field of slaughter. The bird to my left was charged with informing Iffilditch, leader of the Elves, whilst its companion would inform the dwarves that the time was arrived, that the battle long waited for could be commenced.

Slowly I approached the wall lifting my head slightly above its rough parapet of bricks, watching the stalking creatures, seeing only confidence in them as they approached my visible army. My few buried troops, hidden beneath a light coating of dust spread over capes waited, they would rise as one and fire their bows directly into the first ranks of the enemy. Behind them hidden in any cavity or cover they could locate, waited a second and third rank of archers. Horse mounted humans, armed with long steel tipped spears, were hidden in the ruins behind where I stood, they would charge through the archers, spearing into the remaining wall of creatures. Acting as a defense for the first rank of archers, allowing them to retreat and regroup with their comrades, who would be lob firing arrows as fast as they could, arrows which would fly high and long to pierce deep into the attacking ranks forming a trapping wall of dead behind those that had passed forward and were trapped between fire.

Young boys and elves would be carrying fletch after fletch forward to the archers, keeping them supplied, hopefully allowing them to fell rank after rank of the enemy, thus slowing and eventually containing them. Then mounted riders would hack their way back to our lines in a sweeping curve, rallying for another charge as necessary behind the solidly formed line of archers. The enemy would close but only so far, they would retaliate, probably with arrows and spears, many of my defenders would die this day, but still they must hold at all costs.

Once our enemies' forces were engaged, to left and right troops would infiltrate and encircle the battle beleaguered forces, cutting them off from the mountains and any hope of escape. Our aerial forces would target ground troops and engage airborne evil, as and where they could. They would also act as lookouts for the encircling troops, and at the right moment release a flair of dense black smoke, signaling the time for full engagement.

That would be the turning point I hoped; it would also be the moment of greatest risk. The danger to my plan lay in the possibility of not having all of Nailstead's forces drawn out and encircled on the field. There was a potential to have my two waves of troops, having to fight both forwards and backwards against one opposing army that had been split into two. My thoughts turned to a single battle weary Dwarf and the report that he had presented to me three days earlier.

"Sire."

The page bowed low, a habit, that no matter how much I instructed him not to, he could not help himself from doing. He looked excited, much as I now felt. I had viewed the arrival of a dust covered, tired looking dwarf. I had stood watching his path as he had crossed the green sward, which surrounds this, my mountain top castle. I had seen him passed quickly through the first of the gates, where once entered in, he vanished from my sight.

Could it be a full month since I had summoned him to me and given him a challenge to complete, a challenge which might prove to provide the pivotal information I would need to wage a successful war against Nailstead. Stepping away from the window I planned our meeting out in my mind, deep down I felt a growing bout of concern, what news would this brave dwarf bear, what assessments would he have made, if any, I thought, plucking at my chin, wondering.

I ordered the page to fetch mulled beer, adding. "The best we have, this chap deserves at least that for what he has accomplished, if only for returning to us alive!"

My page bowed low, I must stop that, and left me, returning shortly with a flagon mug and a full Crone, which is a dwarf measure made especially for beer. It equates to approximately half a human gallon, dwarves drink heavily but without effect, something that I envy them for being able to do. They also fight and travel, far faster and far harder than men, though not as quickly as elves. Dwarves can appear cumbersome, slow, even dim of wit but they are far from being any of those things. Many battles have been won just because of the presence of dwarves and I think this coming war with Nailstead may well be another such battle.

It took nearly half an hour for the dwarf to be brought to my door, my page entered first to announce him, he then stood aside before leaving and closing the door behind the dwarf. He as was his way stood nonchalantly gnawing on a haunch of freshly cooked meat, seemingly unaware of my presence.

"Welcome, welcome, sit, relax, you look done in, are you ok?" A stupid question really, considering how exhausted he looked. I poured a tankard of beer placing it infront of him. "I see they fed you."

"Call this fed, it is but a light snack!" He expounded, hardly a morsel fit for a dwarf to call a meal.

Typical of his species, bluff and independent, but loyal beyond measure.

"As for how I is, well I arn't shot full of holes, an ifen you don't count the cut on this arm."

He lifted his right arm to show the blood soaked, dust encrusted bandage tied around his wrist.

"Then you could say that I is all-right!"

"Glad to hear it dwarf, and all the more pleased to see you safely returned.

"Thank in you Sire, I is most grateful for your concern and more appreciative for the beer!" He said waving the haunch of half eaten meat in the air around him.

"Any a ways, I ain't here to talk about the weather or me' elf, I guess you might like to know what I discovered, seeing as that was why you sent me and the others out to the mountains and beyond."

'The others', he had said, how did he know that there were others? I had sent three people out to scout, one of each species that made up my people; this dwarf so far was the only one to have returned, as to the others, I am very concerned for them. Now the dwarf talks of them as thought he knows something of their fate.

I know dwarves quite well, as a child and into my formative years I chose their company; it was a dwarf that first taught me the art of self-defense, or 'dwarf tossing' as they call it. It was a dwarf that showed me the intricacies of a variety of weapons. I know them and I respect them, but I also know that they have their own way, a historic way, a way of telling a tale or giving a report and nothing will encourage them to speak out in any other way.

This dwarf before me, would be no different, he would grant me his words in a tale, a story of his deeds and happenings without bluster or self aggrandizement. A dwarf tale can be taken wholly as true and accurate, they make for very poor liars, because of this tale telling, which is all to the good, but it can be frustrating at times. I sat myself comfortably, picked up my glass of elf wine, and readied myself for the tale to come.

The dwarf having finished his meat, downed his beer, then sat up straight, he placed his arms firmly on the chairs arms, he took in a single deep breath and began his story and what a tale it was to be.
